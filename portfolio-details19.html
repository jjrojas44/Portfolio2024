Terminator: Future war

The UI for the terminator: resistance sequel,Terminator: Future war.

Case Study:

Terminator: Future war
Project Overview
  • Project Name: Terminator: Future war (Hypothetical Sequel)
  • Role: UX/UI Designer (Solo Designer)
  • Tools Used: Figma,Photoshop,Photopea,Gameuidatabase,dribbble,Behance,
  • Timeline: 3 Weeks
  • Objective: Introduce a hypothetical UI concept for a sequel called Terminator: Future War.
Value Added

The Terminator: Future War UI concept adds value by creating an immersive, retro-futuristic interface that enhances both gameplay and storytelling.

Problem Statement

Challenges with the Existing UI from Terminator: Resistance:

  • Cluttered HUD and Menus: Players found it difficult to quickly access mission-critical information and objectives during fast-paced combat situations.
  • Weak Thematic Integration: The UI lacked cohesion with the gritty, dystopian tech-driven aesthetic of the Terminator universe, breaking immersion.
  • Poor Visual Hierarchy: Essential gameplay data was often buried or visually overshadowed by less relevant UI elements, reducing usability under pressure.
  • Limited Accessibility Options: The game offered minimal options for HUD scaling, colorblind modes, or UI customization, limiting usability for a diverse player base.

Why a Design for a Hypothetical Sequel?

  • To create a cinematic, immersive, and faction-specific interface that supports storytelling and strengthens narrative worldbuilding.
  • To enhance overall usability with streamlined menus, mission trackers, and clear tactical overlays for improved gameplay flow.
  • To modernize the interface while staying true to the dystopian, war-torn visual identity of the Terminator universe, embracing Skynet-era design language.

Research

Persona

User Insight

To find out what frustrated players most about Terminator: Resistance’s UI, I gathered feedback from game reviews, community forums, and social media. Here’s what stood out:

  • The existing UI felt cluttered and difficult to navigate during combat, which made situational awareness harder than it should be.
  • Players wanted a HUD design that felt like it belonged in the Terminator universe — dark, techy, and retro-futuristic — but without sacrificing usability.
  • Gamers preferred flexible, modular HUD layouts that adapted to the situation (combat vs. exploration) and device (PC vs. console).
  • Inventory management and menus weren’t optimized for consoles or controllers, causing frustration during critical gameplay moments.

What I Learned

  • Immersion matters most — but not at the cost of clear, functional combat UI.
  • Players want scalable, customizable HUD options that shift based on the situation.
  • Other shooter games were handling these problems better, giving me a blueprint for improvement.

POV Statement

The user interface I want should be clean, minimal, and customizable so I can stay fully engaged in both the combat and narrative moments without distraction — while still feeling like it belongs in a gritty, Skynet-controlled future.

How Might We

Based on what I learned, I asked myself:

  • How can I build a clean, faction-themed HUD that stays immersive without overloading the player?
  • How can I simplify menus so they work well on both controllers and keyboard/mouse?
  • How can I visually tie the UI into the Terminator world while keeping it usable?
  • How can I make inventory and status indicators more modular, context-based, and efficient?

Heuristic Evaluation

Purpose: Validate UI concepts against established design heuristics while maintaining consistency with Terminator lore and Skynet-era visuals.

Sources: Analyzed UI systems from Terminator: Dark Fate, Terminator: Salvation, Terminator: Resistance, T-800 HUD film footage, and benchmarked against shooter games like Division 2, Metro Exodus, and Black Ops 6.

Outcome: Developed a franchise-consistent, modular UI style guide for a hypothetical Future War sequel, identifying areas for significant UX improvement.

Evaluation Summary

  • Visibility of System Status: Metro Exodus and Division 2 provided strong situational awareness cues. Terminator: Resistance struggled with real-time danger and status indicators, leaving players uncertain during high-risk moments.
  • Match Between System & Real World: Metro’s immersive in-world UI felt organic, while Black Ops 6 occasionally drifted too abstract. Terminator’s menus felt detached from its narrative world, lacking Skynet-themed contextual design.
  • User Control & Freedom: Division 2 excelled with UI customization options. Terminator: Resistance remained rigid, offering no adjustable HUD elements or layout options for player preference.
  • Consistency & Standards: Black Ops 6 and Division 2 followed clear genre conventions. Terminator’s interface was inconsistent with both genre norms and established Terminator franchise visual standards.
  • Error Prevention: Division 2 proactively warned players before discarding valuable gear or making irreversible choices. Terminator lacked such safeguards, leading to player frustration from lost progress and items.
  • Recognition vs. Recall: Division 2 and Black Ops 6 used intuitive icons and contextual cues to reduce cognitive load. Terminator buried critical information in dense, hard-to-navigate menus without visual hierarchy.
  • Flexibility & Efficiency of Use: Division 2 supported scalable HUD elements and quick-access features. Terminator: Resistance’s UI was static and unadaptable, hindering player efficiency.
  • Aesthetic & Minimalist Design: Metro’s HUD dynamically faded out during exploration, enhancing immersion. Black Ops 6 cluttered mid-combat. Terminator kept unnecessary elements on-screen, lacking modern minimalist refinements.
  • Error Recovery: Division 2 delivered clear, context-specific error messages. Terminator defaulted to generic error notifications without actionable recovery steps or guidance.
  • Help & Documentation: Division 2 incorporated contextual tutorials and onboarding. Metro lacked deeper menu explanations. Terminator’s help and documentation systems were outdated, sparse, and difficult to access mid-game.

Key Takeaways

Strengths: Division 2 leads in UI customization and error prevention. Metro delivers unmatched immersive in-world UI. BO6 retains familiar controls and readability.

Weaknesses: Terminator: Resistance suffers from rigid HUD layouts, inconsistent immersion, and poor error management. BO6 struggles with combat UI clutter.

Opportunities: Add scalable, faction-themed HUD presets in Terminator. Improve contextual tutorials in Metro. Provide an immersive, minimal HUD mode for BO6. Use Division 2’s modular UI system as a benchmark framework.

Competitor Analysis

I analyzed The Division 2, Black Ops 6, Terminator: Resistance, and Metro Exodus, reviewing their HUDs, menus, strengths, weaknesses, and player feedback. This revealed practical fixes for Terminator’s issues, like modular HUDs, better threat indicators, and more intuitive combat menus.

Wireframe & Design Evolution

High Fidelity

Using the feedback gathered from my competitive analysis and heuristic evaluations, I redesigned key inventory and vendor screens to address common player frustrations — like cluttered menus, poor threat visibility, and lack of intuitive item management. These high-fidelity prototypes showcase a modular, grid-based inventory system and streamlined vendor layout, maintaining the gritty, tech-heavy world of Terminator while improving clarity, quick item comparison, and ease of use in combat-heavy moments.

Iteration: First Concept vs. Final Design

My first layout missed the mark. It was too bright, cartoony, and clashed with the gritty Terminator aesthetic. The AI backgrounds, placeholder character, and mobile-style menus felt out of place. Colors and typography didn’t fit the brand. After feedback and comparing to Division 2 and Metro Exodus, I revised the design with a darker palette, clearer threat indicators, cleaner menus, and typography that matched the Skynet-era look.

Iteration: First Iteration vs. Final Iteration

Terminator resistance vs Terminator future war

To see where improvements were made, I did a side-by-side comparison between the original Terminator: Resistance UI and my Future War redesign. The original used static layouts and a bland, slightly futuristic cybernetic style, while mine applied a Skynet-inspired color scheme with a blue hue from the Terminator’s eye socket, futuristic typography, and a clean, stylized header. The new full menu features Map, Operation, Armory, Augmentation, and Outpost, with icons designed in a futuristic, apocalyptic aesthetic to better fit the world’s tone.
